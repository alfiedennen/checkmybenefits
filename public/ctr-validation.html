Council Tax Reduction: Data Validation

Independent verification of MissingBenefit's Council Tax Reduction calculations against official published data

Council Tax Reduction is the single hardest benefit to calculate in the UK. Each of the 296 English councils sets its own working-age scheme, with different rules, caps, and calculation methods. Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland each run nationally prescribed schemes. There is no central database of these local schemes.

MissingBenefit has built a dataset covering all 296 English billing authorities plus the devolved nations. We tested their calculations against official published data to determine whether their Council Tax Reduction estimates are reliable enough to present to citizens.

What we tested

We conducted two types of verification. First, we called the MissingBenefit API for every one of the 296 English billing authorities and compared the Band D rate in each calculation against the official MHCLG dataset. Second, we ran 21 realistic citizen profiles through the API to test the full calculation logic: reduction percentages, means-test arithmetic, pension-age parameters, and scheme rules.

We compared what the API returned against three things:

  1. Published Band D council tax rates, tested for all 296 English councils against official government data
  2. Official council scheme rules, the reduction percentages, caps, and calculation methods each council publishes
  3. National pension-age scheme parameters, the legislated rates set by central government

Band D rates: 296 out of 296 exact

The Band D rate is the foundation of every council tax calculation. If this number is wrong, everything else falls apart. We tested every single one of the 296 English billing authorities by calling the MissingBenefit API with a postcode in each council area, then comparing the Band D rate in the calculation breakdown against the official figure published by the Ministry of Housing, Communities and Local Government (MHCLG).

The result: all 296 matched the official 2025/26 figure to the penny. Not a single discrepancy. This covers every type of authority: 33 London boroughs, 36 metropolitan districts, 63 unitary authorities, and 164 shire districts.

296 out of 296 Band D rates matched exactly. Every English billing authority was tested against the MHCLG's official "Council Tax levels set by local authorities in England 2025 to 2026" dataset. Zero discrepancies.

Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland have nationally prescribed working-age schemes, scored at 90%, 90%, and 70% respectively.

Where council-specific data is unavailable, conservative defaults are used: taper model, 80% maximum reduction, £16,000 capital limit. This means estimates for lower-confidence councils err on the side of understating rather than overstating what someone might receive.

Of the 115 councils using income-banded schemes, 99 have detailed band threshold data available. This means the API can calculate precise per-band reductions rather than applying a single maximum percentage.

Known limitations

These are estimates, not entitlement decisions. Only the local council can make an actual Council Tax Reduction award. These figures help people understand roughly what they could receive and whether it is worth applying.
  • Income precision: We provide income in broad bands (e.g. "under £16,000") rather than exact figures. For taper-based councils, this can shift the estimate significantly. A person earning £8,000 would get substantially more CTR than someone earning £15,000, but both fall in the same band.
  • Band D assumption: Calculations assume the property is in Band D (the median). People in lower bands would pay less; people in higher bands would pay more. Councils with band caps (like Bradford's Band A cap or Sandwell's Band B cap) already limit how much reduction is possible.
  • 229 of 296 councils are at Medium confidence: For these councils, scheme data comes from secondary sources rather than official scheme documents. The parameters may not reflect the most recent changes.
  • Pre-April 2021 pensioners: The personal allowance used (£227.10/week) is for the newer cohort. Pensioners who reached State Pension age before April 2021 have a higher allowance (£244.40), meaning their actual CTR could be slightly higher than shown.
  • No parish precept variation: Some councils include parish precepts in their Band D figure. The API uses the total average Band D, which may differ slightly from the actual charge at a specific address.

Conclusion

MissingBenefit's Council Tax Reduction data is the most comprehensive publicly available dataset of its kind. The foundational data (Band D rates, scheme types, and reduction caps) is demonstrably accurate against official sources. The calculation logic is sound, and the arithmetic checks out.

The main variable affecting accuracy is the precision of the input data (income bands rather than exact figures) and the confidence level for each council's scheme parameters. For High-confidence councils (66 of 296), the estimates should be very close to what a person would actually receive. For Medium-confidence councils, they provide a reasonable indication of eligibility and approximate value.

For citizens trying to understand whether Council Tax Reduction is worth applying for, these estimates are substantially more useful than the current alternative, which is typically no estimate at all, or a vague suggestion to "contact your local council."
